Abdul Qadeer Khan

1 April 1936 _ 10 October 2021

Abdul Qadeer Khan's Early Life and Early Career

At the time of his birth in the mid-1930s, Abdul Qadeer Khan was born into the British Raj, or British India, which included several nation-states such as modern-day India and Pakistan. Khan was born at a time when the nations of India and Pakistan longed to be free of colonial rule. By the early 1940s, World War II was underway, and British India was a critical location for allied forces to combat Japanese forces in southeast Asia. Despite several uprisings within British India, Britain retained power throughout the war. At the conclusion of World War II, Britain was concerned by tensions continuing to grow in India and was aware of religious tensions among Hindus and Muslims. The crown was also concerned about the danger to British citizens from a previous mutiny. As a result, Britain sought to transfer the power of the British Raj to its people swiftly.

Abdul Qadeer Khan and Nuclear Proliferation

In the early 1970s, tensions between Pakistan and India were high. The Eastern portion of Pakistan sought independence from the rest of the nation. Initially, the efforts of the separatists were crushed until India provided support. As a result, Eastern Pakistan gained independence and became the nation of Bangladesh. This defeat was an embarrassment to the nation of Pakistan and increased tensions with India In 1974, India tested its first nuclear weapon, and Pakistani authorities took notice. By September of that year, Khan wrote to Prime Minister Zulfikar Ali Bhutto to share his knowledge and expertise in nuclear weapons development. He encouraged Bhutto to consider a uranium-based nuclear program and not the plutonium-based program that Pakistan was currently pursuing. During this time, Khan had also translated documents concerning more advanced centrifuges from German to Dutch unsupervised by his Dutch employers.
